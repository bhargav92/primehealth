What is Lymph Node Dissection?

Lymph node dissection (also called lymphadenectomy) is a surgical procedure to remove one or more lymph nodes. Lymph nodes are small, bean-shaped glands that are part of the lymphatic system, which helps the body fight infections and filter harmful substances. This procedure is commonly performed to determine whether cancer has spread to the lymph nodes, which can influence treatment options and prognosis.

How to diagnose Lymph Node Dissection? When to consult a doctor?

Diagnosing the need for a lymph node dissection involves several steps: Medical History and Physical Examination, Imaging Tests, Biopsy (Fine Needle Aspiration or Core Biopsy), Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy & Blood Tests.

You should consult a doctor if:

  • Persistent Lymph Node Swelling: If you have swollen lymph nodes that don’t resolve on their own after a few weeks or continue to grow in size.
  • Unexplained Lymph Node Enlargement: If your lymph nodes are enlarged but there is no obvious sign of infection, especially if the nodes are hard, fixed in place, or painless.
  • Pain or Discomfort: If swollen lymph nodes are causing pain or discomfort, especially in regions like the neck, underarms, or groin.
  • Infection or Autoimmune Disorders: If you have signs of a systemic infection or autoimmune disease, such as fever, night sweats, unexplained weight loss, or fatigue, along with swollen lymph nodes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Risks include infection, bleeding, scarring, nerve damage, and the development of lymphedema (swelling caused by fluid buildup due to the removal of lymph nodes).

The number of lymph nodes removed varies depending on the extent of the cancer and the area being treated. In some cases, only a few are removed (sentinel node biopsy), while in others, multiple lymph nodes may be taken out (complete dissection).

Recovery varies depending on the extent of the surgery and the patient’s overall health, but most people can return to normal activities within a few weeks. Full recovery may take longer if complications like lymphedema occur.
